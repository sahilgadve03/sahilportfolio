Vision OS

Spatial UI -Mixed Reality Case Study

Scroll

Embrace curiosity to venture into uncharted design territories, one step at a time, paving the way for fresh and innovative paths in design.

Delving into novel design horizons becomes an exhilarating journey when approached with curiosity and a thirst for knowledge. While some designers may opt for basic glassmorphism card tutorials in spatial design, this can feel monotonous and fail to ignite the spark of creativity.

Start your exploration by defining a full plan of action.

Setting up

My concept involves creating an immersive UI design grading experience. Initially, the idea revolves around a set of five buttons. While there's consideration for potentially transitioning it to a 3D format, it's essential to acknowledge that this decision isn't driven solely by certainty of it being a superior AR experience. Rather, the intention is to embark on this journey with the aim of evolving it into one. Even if this direction doesn't succeed ultimately, the exploration process remains valuable and worthwhile.

Step 1 — immersion in the case study

Despite my initial intention to ground the design in my personal space, I found myself resorting to using images sourced from the web. This departure from authenticity stemmed from challenges in capturing suitable shots at home, compounded by issues with the camera. Consequently, the project's original aim to reflect my genuine surroundings was compromised, leading to a reliance on external imagery that may not fully align with my experience.

Step 2 — Planning

Let's not rush into using Sketch or Figma and slap on a glassmorphic card and call it a day – that would be too simplistic! Instead, I began by sketching out my ideas, emphasizing the importance of maintaining a predominantly flat experience. Introducing excessive layers on the z-axis can overwhelm users cognitively and detract from the overall experience. Therefore, I focused on starting with a single window to showcase the projects, ensuring that simplicity and usability remained at the forefront of the design process.

When designing spatial interfaces, the first principle is familiarity. Rather than reinventing the wheel, I believe in starting with a simple grid of screens submitted by our community members. This approach ensures that users feel comfortable and confident navigating the interface, as they are already familiar with similar layouts.

Next, I proceeded to sketch out the primary vertical set of tabs, which will serve as secondary navigation within the interface. In this project, the main navigation occurs contextually, originating from and operating within the main window. This approach ensures that navigation remains intuitive and seamlessly integrated into the overall user experience.

Continuing with the design process, I envisioned a fully immersive experience upon entering a category of designs. As the user enters the category, the surrounding room dims to create a heightened sense of immersion. A 180° carousel appears in front of the user, allowing them to swipe through different designs, with the main focus consistently centered on the selected design. This approach ensures that users are fully engaged with the showcased designs while maintaining a seamless and immersive experience.

Most of the interface would be completely 2d, but I imagined this interaction of moving designs onto different levels as something that could work well in 3d space.

Main spatial interaction

Having my main “spatial” a-ha moment I decided to add a little bit more detail to the card views.

The card can be rotated around to reveal a low-fidelity wireframe that was used as a base for the UI design. It’s a switch between low fidelity and high-fidelity.

Additionally, I incorporated an additional set of controls to activate hierarchy strips and spacing guidelines used in this design. While this functionality isn't currently available, it's part of our planned features for a future platform version. Implementing it in mixed reality allows users to preview and interact with these features ahead of time. Users can toggle both options on or off individually to gain insight into the UI's structure in greater detail.

Mid-Fidelity

With the plan in mind, I proceeded to sketch the main screens in mid-fidelity to observe how they integrate and flow together. This mid-fidelity approach allows for a detailed examination of the overall design coherence and functionality, ensuring alignment with the envisioned user experience.

The main screen adopts a straightforward layout, presenting a grid of designs accompanied by the author's name and photo. Users are provided with the option to express their appreciation by liking a design. Additionally, I incorporated a filter box to enable users to toggle between viewing only liked projects or all projects, enhancing the browsing experience.

The browsing experience has been enhanced with 3D transformed side-cards, contributing to a visually engaging interface. To prioritize the user's focus on the central card, the side-cards are subtly dimmed. This design choice ensures that the user's attention remains directed towards the card they are currently viewing, fostering a seamless and immersive browsing experience.

When fully focused on the card, it moves closer, revealing extra functionalities, including the option to enable hierarchy strips for enhanced layout insights.

Material Exploration

The next stage involved exploring various materials for the design. While Apple provides its own suggestions and UI kits, I opted to experiment with custom materials to explore new creative directions and gauge their potential impact on the overall design aesthetic. This custom approach allowed for greater flexibility and innovation in the design process.

I pasted my glassmorphic panels onto my room photo and instantly realised it was not going to be that simple. Vision OS adjusts the windows in real-time based on the lighting conditions and general ambience. So I had to modify it to match my space.

It’s definitely clearer and easier to read after those tweaks. The next step was all about fine-tuning the details.

Incorporating the presence of windows on the left side of my room, I adjusted the gradients and outlines in the design to be illuminated from that direction. This subtle modification may go unnoticed consciously, but it engages the brain, enhancing the overall immersive experience. Such attention to detail contributes significantly to the immersion factor of the design, enriching the user's interaction with the interface.

Mapping interactions

Initially, I envisioned the swiping and flicking interaction in 3D space, with the card moving along a circular path. However, upon reflection, I realized that having two separate 3D planes of movement was overwhelming and potentially confusing for users. Therefore, I revised the concept to focus solely on horizontal movement in space, simplifying the interaction to a swipe up/down in three dimensions. This adjustment maintains the immersive experience while ensuring clarity and ease of use for the user.

My Thoughts?

It's reminiscent of Steve Jobs' approach to introducing new categories of devices, emphasizing their specific advantages over existing technologies. Considering whether the mixed reality experience offers distinct benefits compared to using a phone or tablet app is crucial. It's essential to assess whether the immersive nature of mixed reality enhances the user's ability to analyze and interact with the designs effectively. Your idea of creating an environment conducive to focused analysis by dimming distractions and enlarging the design in real space demonstrates a thoughtful approach to optimizing the user experience. Further exploration and potential development of the experience on the actual device will provide valuable insights into its effectiveness and usability.
